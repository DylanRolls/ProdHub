🔴 The Kick Drum

The kick is your gravity anchor. It tells your body where the floor is. In DnB/Jungle, kicks often land on beats 1 and 3 (steps 1 and 9 in 16ths), but offbeat and syncopated kicks create the rolling, hypnotic feel.

WHY The kick sits in the sub-low range (50–80Hz punch, sub boom at 40–60Hz). When it hits on a downbeat, it confirms the groove. When it hits off the downbeat, it creates forward momentum — that's what makes jungle feel like it's always "about to drop."
🟡 The Snare / Clap

Snare almost always lands on beats 2 and 4 (steps 5 and 13) in most EDM. This is the backbeat — it's what you clap to. In jungle/DnB, snares are sometimes rolled, ghost-noted, or pushed off the grid slightly for swing.

WHY The backbeat on 2 and 4 is hardwired into human rhythm perception. It creates call-and-response with the kick. Moving it forward (rushing) creates tension. Delaying it creates a lazy, swinging feel.
🟢 Hi-Hats

Hats fill the space between kicks and snares. Closed hats on every 8th note (steps 1,3,5,7...) create steady momentum. Open hats on the "and" of beats (steps 3, 7, etc.) create swing and breathe. In jungle, hats are often syncopated and rolled.

WHY Hats live in the 8–12kHz range. They are the "clock" of your groove. Varying the hat velocity (quieter on off-beats, louder on on-beats) creates that human, breathing feel — this is called velocity automation.
🟠 Percussion & Ghost Notes

Ghost notes are very quiet hits between main snare hits. They add humanity and groove. In DnB/Jungle, percussion like shakers, rimshots, and bongos on off-beats creates that polyrhythmic layering that makes beats feel alive.

WHY Multiple rhythmic layers create a gestalt — the brain hears each layer individually AND as a combined pattern. This is why great jungle breaks feel incredibly dense but not cluttered.

⚡ Syncopation

Hitting between the main beats (on the "ands" and "es"). This is what makes jungle feel like it's tumbling forward. The brain expects a hit and doesn't get it — then gets a surprise hit instead. That tension→surprise is groove.

🔄 Polyrhythm

Two rhythms running at different rates simultaneously. A hat pattern in 3 against a kick in 4 creates a pattern that repeats every 12 beats. This is common in electrofunk and Afrobeat-influenced EDM. GRiZ uses this constantly.

🧲 Swing / Shuffle

Pushing even-numbered 16th notes slightly late. This creates the "drunk walking" feel of funk and jazz. In Ableton, use the Groove Pool → drag a MPC60 or Swing groove onto your MIDI clip. Start at 50% swing.

🌊 Half-Time vs Full-Time

At 160BPM, a full-time snare on beats 2&4 feels frantic. A half-time snare on beat 3 only (step 9) makes 160BPM feel like a slow, heavy 80BPM — that's neurofunk and trap at high BPMs. This is a key DnB technique.

🎯 The Amen Break — Why Jungle Sounds Like Jungle

The most sampled break in history. It's a 4-bar loop with a kick, snare, and hat that are all slightly swung and syncopated. The magic:

  • The kick hits on unexpected 16th-note positions
  • The snare is pushed slightly ahead of beat 3 (creating urgency)
  • Ghost notes on the snare make it feel like a live drummer
  • Pitched up to 160–175BPM, the swing gets exaggerated → jungle "roll" feel
YOUR ACTION IN ABLETON Load a break sample into a Simpler in "Classic" mode. Use warp markers at 170BPM. Then cut it into slices using "Slice to new MIDI track" — now you can rearrange individual drum hits to build your own rolling patterns.
🧠 Why Kick Placement Matters — Neurologically

The low-frequency bass of a kick drum is felt as much as heard — sub frequencies below 80Hz are processed partly by the somatosensory system (touch). This is why clubs have big subs: it's a physical, not just auditory, experience.

When the kick hits on a predictable downbeat, the brain releases dopamine at the moment of confirmation. When it hits just slightly off the expected beat (syncopation), the brain gets a small dopamine spike from the surprise. That's groove.

THE SCIENCE Research in music cognition (Grahn & Brett, 2007) shows the basal ganglia — the brain's rhythm center — fires in sync with beats. Syncopation activates this system MORE than a straight beat because it requires prediction + correction. That extra brain activity = the feeling of being "locked in."
Bass & Frequencies
What to play, where to play it, and why it hits hard
🔊 The Golden Rule of Bass Bass frequencies carry the emotional weight of your track. Sub-bass is felt. Mid-bass is heard. Together they create a physical experience. In DnB and jungle, bass is as melodic as any instrument — but it also functions as rhythm, texture, and identity all at once.
Frequency Map — Click any range to learn more
🎸 Sub Bass (20–80Hz)

This is the physical punch. In Serum 2, use a sine wave or triangle for your sub layer. Keep it simple — one or two notes. Sub bass rarely moves around. It sits under the whole track like a foundation.

Best notes for sub: C1, D1, F1, G1 — these sit in the sweet spot where subs are felt on big systems without becoming mud.

⚡ ABLETON TIP Use a Utility device on your sub channel and set it to Mono. Sub frequencies below 80Hz should always be mono — stereo sub causes phase cancellation and loses energy on club systems.
🎛 Mid-Bass (80–300Hz)

This is where basslines are heard rather than felt. Reese basses, wobble basses, and growl basses live here. In Serum 2: start with a Wavetable with detuned saws, apply a bandpass filter sweeping 100–400Hz for movement.

Classic Neurofunk bass: two oscillators detuned 5–10 cents apart with heavy distortion and a movement LFO on the filter cutoff synced to the beat.

⚡ ABLETON TIP Layer your sub (sine, mono) and mid-bass (wavetable, can be stereo) on separate tracks. Use a high-pass filter at 80Hz on the mid-bass to let the sub breathe. This is called "bass layering."
🎵 What Notes to Use for Basslines

Your bassline should almost always follow the root notes of your chords or key. If you're in A minor, the safest bass notes are: A, C, D, E, G. The most powerful movement is from the root (A) up to the fifth (E) — this is the basis of most bass riffs in every genre of music.

RHYTHM OF THE BASSLINE The most powerful thing you can do for a bassline is lock it to the kick. When the kick and bass hit together, it's called "kick-bass lock" — it creates a feeling of unstoppable forward momentum. In DnB, the bass often mirrors the kick pattern with held notes between the kicks for continuity.
🏗️ How to Actually Build a Bassline — Step by Step

Most beginners try to write the bassline and drums at the same time. The pros do it in layers, and there's a specific order that makes everything click together.

1
LOCK THE ROOT NOTE TO THE KICK

Start with just one note — the root of your key. Place it exactly where your kick hits. Every kick hit gets a bass note. Now play it back. That single note transforms the kick from a drum into a musical statement. The kick and bass become one object.

2
ADD HELD NOTES BETWEEN KICKS

Extend your root note so it rings out between kicks as a long held note. The bass now fills the space and creates continuity — it connects the rhythmic hits. The groove becomes a texture, not just a pulse. This is the most important bassline technique in DnB.

3
INTRODUCE A SECOND NOTE — THE FIFTH

Now take the last kick of the bar and change its bass note to the fifth of the scale (e.g. A→E in A minor). Suddenly the bassline is moving. It asks a question on beat 4 and answers it on beat 1 of the next bar. This is the root-fifth movement that drives nearly every bass riff ever written.

4
ADD RHYTHMIC VARIATION — OFF-BEAT HITS

Add a short bass hit on a 16th note that doesn't have a kick under it. This is your syncopated "ghost" bass hit. It fills the space between the kick-locked notes and adds the forward-pushing feel of jungle and funk. Keep it short — a 16th note long — and quieter than the main hits.

5
ADD TONAL MOVEMENT — PASSING NOTES

On one of those off-beat ghost hits, use the minor 3rd or 4th instead of the root. These are called passing notes — they create a sense of walking from one stable note to the next. In A minor: an Eb or D between an A and an E sounds immediately like electrofunk bass. This single technique is responsible for 80% of all bassline "flavour."

🤖 Integrating Unnatural Synth Sounds With Traditional Drums

This is the real challenge of DnB, neurofunk, and experimental electrofunk — your bass sounds like an alien, but your drums are still basically human. The tension between those two things is what creates the genre. Here's how to make it work instead of clash.

⚡ PRINCIPLE 1: The Bass Is a Rhythm Instrument First

No matter how insane your growl, reese, or talking bass sounds, it must still hit on the beat. The rhythm of the bass notes must be comprehensible even if the timbre is alien. If the rhythmic relationship between bass and kick is clear, the brain can accept any amount of tonal weirdness.

Think of it this way: the drums are the skeleton. The synth bass is the flesh and skin. You can make the skin look like anything — but the skeleton underneath has to hold the shape.

🎚 PRINCIPLE 2: Sidechain Compression — The Glue

The single most important technique for integrating synth bass with drums. When the kick hits, a compressor ducks the bass volume slightly and then lets it come back up. This creates space for the kick to punch through and then the bass fills back in — a pumping, breathing relationship between the two elements.

In Ableton: On your bass track, add a Compressor. Click the sidechain arrow and select your kick as the input. Set attack to 1–5ms (fast, catches the kick), release to 80–150ms (lets bass return before next kick). Threshold around -20dB. Ratio 4:1. You'll hear the bass "pump" with every kick. That's the sound.

⚡ ADVANCED: Multi-band Sidechain Only sidechain the sub frequencies of your bass (use LFO Tool or a parallel chain with a low-pass filter before the compressor). This way only the sub pumps — the mid-bass growl stays constant. The result: rhythmic clarity in the lows without losing the body of your sound.
🔬 PRINCIPLE 3: Transient Alignment — The Click

Synth bass sounds often have slow attacks — the sound grows in over 20–50ms rather than hitting instantly. When this overlaps with the kick's transient, they blur together and both lose definition. The fix is to make sure your bass's amplitude envelope has a fast attack on the notes that hit with the kick.

In Serum 2: set your Amp envelope attack to 0–5ms for kick-aligned notes. For held notes between kicks, you can use a longer attack for a smoother, swelling quality. You can automate this per-note using MIDI CC or by drawing envelope automation in Ableton on the bass track.

🎯 PRINCIPLE 4: Rhythmic Counterpoint — Bass Fills The Gaps

The most sophisticated basslines in neurofunk and jungle aren't just playing with the kick — they're playing against it. Where the kick has silence, the bass moves. Where the bass holds, the kick hits. This creates a back-and-forth conversation: two voices interweaving instead of both saying the same thing at the same time.

Practical exercise: In Ableton, lay out your kick pattern. Now open your bass MIDI clip and only allow bass movement on steps that have NO kick. The result forces a counterpoint relationship and immediately sounds more sophisticated than a bass that just doubles the kick.

🌀 PRINCIPLE 5: Filter Movement = Rhythmic Identity

In neurofunk and electrofunk, the filter movement IS the rhythm. A static bass note that has its filter opening and closing in sync with the beat is perceived as rhythmically active even when the pitch isn't changing. This is how a held whole-note bass can feel as dynamic as a drum roll.

In Serum 2: Assign LFO1 to Filter Cutoff. Set LFO1 to sync to your DAW tempo. Try 1/4, 1/8, or 1/16 rates. A 1/8th-note LFO on the filter with the bass holding a root note creates a "wah-wah" rhythm that locks perfectly with the drums. This is the electrofunk vocabulary in its most direct form.

⚡ ABLETON: AUTOMATE THE LFO RATE Draw automation on the Serum 2 macro knob you've mapped to LFO Rate. Switch it from 1/4 to 1/8 to 1/16 across the track. The filter rhythm doubles in speed and creates a feeling of accelerating energy — perfect for builds.
🎭 Genre Breakdown — How Bass Relates to Drums by Style
NEUROFUNK / DnB

Relationship: Bass and kick are almost inseparable. The bass is the melody. The kick is a trigger for the bass movement — every kick lands with a bass hit, and the bass continues mutating and modulating between kicks.

The key technique: Program the bass to morph its timbre continuously. The kick confirms the beat. The bass makes the beat sound alien. The snare on beat 3 (half-time) creates the heaviness that makes room for the bass to be complex and loud without overwhelming.

JUNGLE / BREAKBEAT

Relationship: The drums are chaotic (syncopated break), so the bass must be more anchored and clear. The bass typically holds long root notes through the complexity of the break and moves only occasionally. This contrast is what makes the rolling drums feel energetic — the bass is the calm center of the storm.

The key technique: Keep bass movement to 1–2 note changes per bar. Let the drums be the complex voice. The contrast between chaotic drums and anchored bass is the defining tension of jungle.

ELECTROFUNK / GRIZ STYLE

Relationship: The drums are grounded and funky (kick on 1, syncopated kick on the "and" of 3, snare on 2 and 4). The bass is conversational — it responds to and answers the drums melodically. Think of a funk bassist reacting to a drummer in real time.

The key technique: Build your bass riff around the spaces in the kick pattern. The kick says something. The bass responds. The kick says something again. The bass finishes the sentence. This call-and-response structure is the heart of electrofunk and the thing that makes GRiZ sound like a live band even when it isn't.

CLOZEE / PSYCHEDELIC BASS MUSIC

Relationship: The drums are often simpler and more tribal (four-on-the-floor kick or a slower half-time pattern). The bass is allowed to be wildly organic and melodic. The bass and lead synth often blur — it's not always clear which is which.

The key technique: Use the bass as a textural instrument as much as a rhythmic one. Let it breathe and evolve. Use long held notes with heavy filter modulation. The simplicity of the drums creates the space for the bass to be expressive and free. Less is more in the rhythm, which means the bass can do more.

🏛️ Bass Architecture — Thinking in Layers

Professional bass music doesn't have "a bass" — it has a bass architecture made of 3–4 distinct layers that each do a different job. Understanding these layers changes how you hear and build everything.

Layer 1: The Sub

Sine wave, 40–80Hz. Mono. Never moves much. This is the foundation. It doesn't need to be interesting — it needs to be present and consistent. You feel this layer, you don't really hear it.

Layer 2: The Body

Reese or growl, 80–300Hz. This is the warmth and weight you hear in headphones. It carries the rhythmic identity of the bassline. This is the layer that breathes with the kick via sidechain.

Layer 3: The Presence

The upper harmonic content of your synth — 300Hz–2kHz. This is the growl, the vowel movement, the "talking" quality. This layer is where all the interesting Serum 2 modulation lives. It's what makes people say "what IS that sound?"

Layer 4: The Air (Optional)

High-passed distortion, 2kHz+. Adds definition, edge, and excitement on big systems. Often a separate parallel distortion chain with heavy saturation and a high-pass filter. This layer makes the bass cut through a full mix.

THE PRACTICAL WORKFLOW In Ableton, set up 3 tracks for your bass: Sub (sine, Utility mono), Body (Serum 2 main patch, high-passed at 80Hz), and Presence (the same Serum 2 patch on a parallel chain with heavy distortion and high-passed at 300Hz). Group all three. Solo each layer as you build. This is how professional DnB bass sound design works.

🔄 How Counter-Melody Works — Visual Breakdown

A counter-melody isn't random — it follows three laws that make it complement rather than fight the main melody. The piano rolls below show each law in action.

Law 1: Fill the Silences

The counter-melody (red) only plays during rests in the main melody (green). They never overlap. This is call-and-response — the melody speaks, then the counter answers in the silence. Listen to any GRiZ track and you'll hear this everywhere.

Law 2: Move in Opposite Direction

When the main melody goes up, the counter moves down. When the main descends, the counter ascends. This "contrary motion" creates a braided, interlocking texture — two voices weaving around each other. It's the most sophisticated sound in melody writing.

Law 3: Different Rhythm, Same Key

The counter-melody uses a different rhythmic pattern than the main melody. Where the main has long notes, the counter uses quick 16th-note runs. Where the main is rapid, the counter sustains. Rhythmic contrast makes both melodies sound more interesting than either would alone.

🎯 Motif — The Hook Building Block

A motif is a short 2–4 note idea that repeats and transforms throughout your track. It's how melodies become memorable. The same 3 notes can sound completely different when you vary the rhythm, the octave, or the starting point.

The Same 3-Note Motif — 4 Transformations
CLOZEE + ELECTRIC MANTIS TECHNIQUE Take a 3-note motif. Play it forward. Then play it backward (retrograde). Then transpose it up a 4th. Each transformation is the same motif but sounds fresh. String these together across 8 bars and you have a complete melodic development that feels inevitable, not random.
🎯 Melody Construction Rules
  • Start and end on the root note — creates resolution
  • Use mostly steps (adjacent notes), leap occasionally for drama
  • Peak once — highest note should happen once, at the climax
  • Repeat motifs — a 2-4 note idea that comes back (hooks)
  • Leave space — silence IS melody. Let notes breathe.
🔄 Counter-Melody Rules
  • Move opposite — main goes up, counter goes down
  • Fill silences — counter lives in the gaps
  • Different register — main in mids, counter in highs
  • Same key, different rhythm — syncopate it
  • Clozee: main in pad, counter in plucked synth with stereo motion
🎛 Translating Melody to Serum 2
LEAD SYNTH (Clozee-style organic lead)

🧠 The Science of the Drop

The "drop" triggers a measurable neurological response. Research (Salimpoor et al., 2011) shows that anticipated musical moments — especially release after tension — cause a dopamine spike in the nucleus accumbens. This is why drops feel physically euphoric.

  • Strip elements before the drop (remove bass, reduce to hats + riser only)

Think of your track's energy as a waveform with peaks and troughs. Constant high energy = listener fatigue. Every high needs a low to contrast it.

🗂 Session View vs Arrangement View

Session View (the grid of clips) is for composing and jamming — try ideas, loop sections, build patterns freely. Arrangement View (the timeline) is for structuring and finishing your track.

Workflow: Build your loops in Session View → when you're happy, hit Record in Arrangement View while jamming your Session → you now have a rough arrangement to edit and refine.

  • Use Simpler in Classic mode for break samples
  • Groove Pool: drag MPC60 groove at 50–70% for swing
  • Automate velocity on hats (alt-click velocity lane in MIDI editor)
  • Use "Slice to MIDI" on breaks to chop and rearrange

🎚 Mixing Fundamentals for DnB/Jungle
KICK
Keep sub punchy with a transient shaper. High-pass everything else at 40Hz. Side-chain compress the bass to the kick (LFO Tool or Ableton Compressor with sidechain input from kick).
BASS
Sub layer: mono, no FX, high-pass at 40Hz. Mid-bass: stereo ok, high-pass at 80Hz. Keep total bass peak at -6dB on master. Use a limiter on bass bus to catch transients.
LEADS
High-pass at 300Hz (let bass own the lows). Add subtle saturation (Saturator in Ableton) for harmonics. Use EQ to carve out space so leads don't clash with bass or hats.
HATS/PERC
High-pass at 2kHz. Keep peaks at -12dB so they sit above everything without harshness. Use a wide stereo spread on hats but keep snare and kick centered (mono below 200Hz).
🚀 Your First Track — Step by Step
  1. Set your BPM (try 170 for jungle). Set your key (A minor is classic DnB territory).
  2. Make a 1-bar drum loop first — just kick + snare. Get the groove right before adding anything else.
  3. Add hats to the drum loop. Experiment with the groove pool.
  4. Add a simple sub bass line (2–4 notes, root and fifth, locks to kick)
  5. Add a mid-bass layer in Serum 2
  6. Build a 4-bar loop of just drums + bass. Listen and feel it.
  7. Add a simple 2-bar lead melody (4–6 notes max, in your key)
  8. Add a counter melody or chord pad on top
  9. Start building your arrangement: intro → build → drop → breakdown → drop 2 → outro
  10. Add automation: filter sweeps, volume rides, riser effects before drops
ADHD TIP Work in 25-minute blocks with a timer (Pomodoro). Start with drums EVERY time — it's the most immediate, tactile, gratifying part. Let the groove pull you in. If you're stuck on a melody, switch to drums. The beat will unlock the melody.
